\begin{center}
{\Large\bfseries ABSTRACT}
\end{center}

\vspace{1cm}

This study develops and validates a drought-adjusted, two-regime Markov-switching credit migration model for estimating Expected Credit Loss (ECL) in Zimbabwean Microfinance Institutions (MFIs). The research addresses the critical methodological gap between the forward-looking requirements of IFRS 9 and the static, time-homogeneous credit risk models currently employed by climate-vulnerable financial institutions in Sub-Saharan Africa. Using quarterly credit transition data from Zimbabwean MFIs paired with Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI) drought indicators, the study estimates two distinct migration matrices---one for normal/wet conditions and one for drought/stress conditions---with regime probabilities determined by an exogenous logistic function of the drought index. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) is employed to calibrate model parameters, while bootstrap inference provides standard errors and confidence intervals. A Likelihood Ratio Test (LRT) formally tests the significance of regime differentiation against a single-matrix null hypothesis. The resulting model produces dynamic, climate-conditioned Probability of Default (PD) estimates and IFRS 9-compliant ECL forecasts under multiple drought scenarios. The research contributes to both academic literature on climate-financial risk integration and practical risk management frameworks for emerging market microfinance institutions.

\vspace{1cm}

\noindent\textbf{Key Words:} Expected Credit Loss, IFRS 9, Markov-Switching Model, Credit Migration Matrix, Drought Risk, Microfinance, Zimbabwe, Climate Risk, Probability of Default
